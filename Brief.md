## 1) Header

This should be a slim, sticky nav with very little visual weight. Use the logo on the left, 3–5 nav anchors in the center or right, and one primary CTA button. For Daemon, the nav should feel operational: Platform, Intelligence, Audits, Research, and Contact.

Keep the header slightly translucent or bordered so it sits above the page without stealing attention. On mobile, collapse to a clean menu drawer with the CTA still visible.

## 2) Hero

This is the most important section and should be the heaviest visual moment on the page. The template’s hero is designed for a full-bleed shader background, which fits your Palantir/Anduril direction very well.[^2_1]

Recommended hero layout:

- Left side: headline, subheadline, CTAs, and small trust metrics.
- Right or center: wireframe 3D model, ASCII dither shader, or system visualization.
- Background: subtle grid, scanlines, or moving noise.
- Supporting line: one sentence that explains the platform in plain language.

For Daemon, the hero should communicate: multi-agent blockchain security, explainability, compliance, and real-time threat intelligence.[^2_2]

## 3) Value Props

The three-up value prop grid should be used to break Daemon into its core modules. This is where the user first understands the product pillars without reading the whitepaper.[^2_2]

Suggested cards:

- Cyclops: Analyst-facing visualization and observability.
- Caraxes: Smart contract auditing subsystem.
- Threat Intelligence: Cross-chain, anomaly, and fraud detection.
- Compliance: AML, sanctions, and audit trail support.

Each card should have a short title, a one-line explanation, and one technical metric or outcome.

## 4) Logo Marquee

The vertical logo marquee in the template is ideal for trust-building. Use it to show integrations, data sources, and ecosystem coverage.[^2_1]

Good logo groups:

- Chains: Solana, Ethereum, EVM L2s.
- Data providers: Helius, Solscan, Etherscan.
- Threat intel: ScamSniffer, GoPlus, community datasets.
- Security tools: Slither, Mythril, Echidna.

This section should feel like infrastructure credibility, not marketing.

## 5) How It Works

The step list is where you explain the pipeline in a simple, linear way. The whitepaper already gives you a progressive analysis flow, so this section can mirror that structure.[^2_2]

Recommended steps:

1. Ingest on-chain activity and address history.
2. Enrich with threat intel and community datasets.
3. Run multi-agent analysis across behavior, graph, anomaly, and exploit signals.
4. Synthesize explainable findings and risk scores.
5. Publish to Cyclops and export audit-ready reports.

Keep each step short, with one supporting sentence and a small visual cue.

## 6) Testimonials / Proof

Even if you do not have customer testimonials yet, this section can be reframed as proof statements, validation quotes, or benchmark cards. The template includes a testimonial carousel, so it can support social proof or technical proof equally well.[^2_1]

For Daemon, use:

- Benchmark stats from the whitepaper.
- Short quotes from research context.
- One or two “validated by” or “built for” statements.
- If no customers exist yet, use research-grade proof blocks instead.


## 7) Showcase Carousel

This is the best place to show product screenshots, diagrams, or feature cards. In your case, the carousel should present:

- Cyclops dashboard.
- Caraxes audit flow.
- Transaction graph visualization.
- Alert and risk report UI.

Use this as the “see the product” section, not a generic gallery. Each card should have a title, a short caption, and one key outcome.

## 8) Community Section

The pinned community block is important because your whitepaper emphasizes incentive design and community intelligence. This section should describe how researchers, auditors, and contributors participate in the ecosystem.[^2_2]

Possible content:

- Threat submissions.
- Dataset contributions.
- Vulnerability validation.
- Governance participation.
- Reward mechanisms.

Visually, this can be a darker callout section with one strong diagram or a simple flow showing contribution to intelligence to reward.

## 9) Pricing

If you are not ready to expose pricing, use this section as an access model or deployment model instead. The template’s three-tier pricing structure can become:

- Research.
- Team.
- Enterprise.

Or:

- Pilot.
- Growth.
- Mission-critical.

If you want to avoid pricing entirely for now, convert the cards into “deployment options” or “engagement tiers.”

## 10) FAQ

The FAQ should remove friction and answer the obvious enterprise questions. Focus on security, data sources, supported chains, explainability, deployment model, and compliance. This section should be concise but direct.

Strong FAQ topics:

- What chains are supported?
- Is the analysis explainable?
- Can it run in private environments?
- How does Cyclops differ from Caraxes?
- What threat intel sources are used?
- How is accuracy validated?


## 11) Final CTA

The final CTA should be very clean and confident. It should close the page with a single message: request a demo, access the research, or start a pilot.

For Daemon, the CTA can say:

- “Deploy intelligence that sees across chains.”
- Button: Request a demo.
- Secondary: Read the architecture.


## 12) Footer

The footer should stay technical and minimal. Include product links, research links, docs, contact, and possibly social or GitHub. Keep it structured like an infrastructure company, not a consumer startup.

A good footer layout:

- Product.
